Introduction to turtle conservation in Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka is home to a diverse range of turtle species, including the critically endangered Hawksbill and Green sea turtles. However, these populations are under threat due to habitat loss, pollution, and overfishing. To address this issue, the Sri Lankan government has established several turtle hatcheries around the country to protect these vulnerable species. These hatcheries not only provide a safe place for turtle eggs to hatch and for hatchlings to grow, but they also conduct research and conservation efforts to ensure the survival of these turtles in their natural habitats. By visiting these hatcheries, tourists can learn about the importance of turtle conservation in Sri Lanka and contribute to the efforts to protect these amazing creatures.

Types of Turtles found in Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka is a key area for sea turtle conservation efforts because it is home to five of the world's seven species of sea turtles.

  • Green sea turtle
  • Olive Ridley turtle
  • Hawksbill turtle
  • Leatherback turtle
  • Loggerhead turtle

The Green sea turtle and the Olive Ridley turtle, both of which are categorized as endangered on the IUCN Red List, are the two turtle species that are most frequently encountered in Sri Lanka. Although much less common, the Hawksbill, Leatherback, and Loggerhead turtles can also be found in Sri Lankan waters. Due to the demand for its precious shell, which is frequently used to create jewelry and other decorative products, hawksbill turtles in particular are in grave risk of extinction.


The majority of Sri Lanka's turtle species are green sea turtles, which are thought to nest on the nation's beaches on a yearly basis in numbers between 5,000 and 10,000. These turtles can weigh up to 200 kilograms and reach lengths of up to 1.2 meters. With an estimated 4,000 to 5,000 nesting each year, Olive Ridley turtles are also prevalent in Sri Lanka. These turtles can reach lengths of up to 70 cm and weights of up to 50 kilograms, making them slightly smaller than Green sea turtles. The biggest sea turtles, known as leatherbacks, are uncommon in Sri Lanka and are not frequently spotted on the beaches of the country.

Why turtle hatcheries are important in Sri Lanka?

In Sri Lanka, turtle populations are greatly protected by turtle hatcheries. The fact that hatcheries offer a secure and controlled environment for turtle eggs to hatch and for hatchlings to grow is one of the key reasons why they are vital. Without hatcheries, natural predators like dogs, birds, and other animals would have a chance to eat turtle eggs. Hatcheries relocate the eggs to a secure location and care for them until they hatch in order to safeguard them from these predators. Until they are healthy enough to survive on their own in the water, the hatchlings are held in the hatchery after the eggs have hatched. This procedure substantially raises turtle survival rates, which is essential for preserving a healthy turtle population in Sri Lanka.

Turtle hatcheries in Sri Lanka are essential to conservation and research efforts in addition to safeguarding turtle eggs and hatchlings. Research on turtle behavior, nesting practices, and other elements that influence turtle populations is frequently done in hatcheries. This study aids in the improvement of conservationists' comprehension of turtle needs and the creation of successful turtle protection plans. In order to educate locals and tourists about the value of turtle protection, hatcheries regularly run educational programs. Tourists can assist in conservation efforts by learning about the difficulties facing Sri Lanka's turtle numbers by visiting hatcheries. Overall, turtle hatcheries are essential for preserving these incredible creatures for future generations and for keeping healthy turtle populations in Sri Lanka.

Different turtle hatcheries in Sri Lanka

There are various turtle hatcheries in Sri Lanka, and they are crucial to preserving and protecting the turtle population.Some of the popular turtle hatcheries are;

  1. Kosgoda turtle hatchery
  2. Hikkaduwa turtle hatchery
  3. Benthota turtle hatchery
  4. Urawatte Sea turtle hatchery
  5. Telwatta turtle hatchery
  6. Sea turtle hatchery Habaraduwa

The Hikkaduwa Turtle Hatchery, which is situated on Sri Lanka's southwest coast, is one of the well-known hatcheries. Since its establishment in 1986, the hatchery has played a significant role in the preservation of a number of turtle species. Several turtle species, notably the critically endangered Hawksbill and Green turtles, call the Hikkaduwa hatchery home. Visitors can observe newborn turtles being released into the ocean at the hatchery, learn about the various turtle species that can be found in Sri Lanka, and even adopt a turtle to aid in conservation efforts.

The Bentota Turtle Hatchery is another well-known turtle breeding facility in Sri Lanka. The hatchery, which is situated on Sri Lanka's southwest coast, was founded in 1981 and is committed to the preservation of many turtle species, including the Green turtle and the Loggerhead turtle. A group of qualified conservationists work at the hatchery to safeguard and monitor turtle nests as well as to inform tourists about the need to save turtles. Overall, Sri Lanka's turtle hatcheries are an essential element of the country's unique natural heritage and serve a significant role in safeguarding and conserving turtle populations.
